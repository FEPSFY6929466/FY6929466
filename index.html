Abstract

In the computer industry, performance has traditionally been the main focus. This has led to computer hardware being less energy efficient due to chasing performance gains by increasing power draw. Power-efficient hardware carries a lot of benefits which are often overlooked, including being more environmentally friendly, due to lower energy demand. This is especially more relevant as the demand for energy from datacentres has increased as a result of AI. Power-efficiency allows for more practicality by enabling longer battery life and thinner portable devices. Furthermore, lower operating voltages improve reliability by reducing degradation, such as by the means of electromigration, as seen in recent failures of intel 13th/14th generation processors. This study highlights the fact that power efficiency should be more of a focus than it currently is and concludes that prioritizing power efficiency allows for computing to be more sustainable, practical and reliable.

Introduction

Power efficiency is often an overlooked area in computing. This is because the highest level of performance is achieved when higher amounts of power are supplied to the system. Manufacturers are constantly competing to deliver the fastest product, and as advancements in computer silicone slow down, manufacturers are also looking at alternative ways of improving performance; this results in high-power computer hardware being the priority. While giving computer hardware more power may seem the best because of the performance gains, it comes with its own set of drawbacks, such as a greater environmental impact, more heat and, depending on the application, a loss in practicality. This article will explore why power-efficient computers are important and why this aspect of computer hardware shouldn’t be overlooked.

Environmental Importance

With the ever-growing presence of global warming and the constant use of computers in our day-to-day operations, it is essential that we understand the impacts that computers have on our environment, varying from their energy usage to the footprint they leave during their manufacturing.
Having more energy efficient computers will reduce the demand for energy which will lead to less greenhouse gases emitted into the atmosphere. This is because the majority – about 85% - of the earth’s energy is produced using forms of non-renewable energy (Cynthia Louise Grier , 2025). This is especially important now due to the expansion of datacentres to meet the demand for AI. Energy demand from datacentres has risen by around 12% per year since 2017, according to IEA (2025).
Additionally, computers which consume more energy are typically more complex to manufacture and require more raw materials due to the need for more advanced cooling systems which furthers the environmental impact. An estimated 70% of the energy required to make and operate a computer during its lifespan is used in the manufacturing process (Arizona State University, 2011).

Reliability & Stability

Furthermore, a more power efficient computer is more likely to have a longer lifespan and exhibit greater stability, making the system more reliable. This is due to less heat output because of the system consuming less power. Heat shortens the lifespan of computer components by causing material degradation at a micro-level. This leads to physical and chemical changes that cause eventual failure.
A method that manufacturers use to maximise performance is to increase the operating voltage of computer hardware in an attempt to enable them to draw greater amounts of power. By increasing the voltage, this can potentially lead to the degradation of the computer silicon through the means of electromigration; this is the movement of atoms in the metal interconnects within the silicon. This happens when the current density is very high due to more voltage, so electrons flowing through a conductor physically push metal atoms out of place, which, over time, creates gaps or buildups on these metal interconnects, which breaks the circuit, causing instability or failure over time. A major example of this can be seen with the recent intel 13th and 14th generation computer processor failures which were a result of too high of a voltage being run through the circuitry (Intel, 2024) . It is important that computer hardware is as stable as possible to prevent downtime, which can lead to the economic drawback of having to replace faulty hardware and the loss of data. Power efficient hardware, which draw lower voltages, is more reliable and is significantly less likely to lead to failure.

Conclusion

Ultimately, power-efficient computers should be more of a focus than they are currently, rather than hardware being allowed to use as much power as possible being the priority; it is shown there are diminishing performance returns. Power-efficiency reduces environmental harm by reducing energy demand, improves practicality on portable devices through longer battery life and thinner devices, and improves reliability by protecting hardware from instability and degradation. These benefits demonstrate that efficiency is important in computing and will further continue to become more important with the increasing growth of AI and the presence of climate change.
